# Services: The New Software

> **Source:** [services-the-new-software.md](../sources/services-the-new-software.md) — Sequoia Capital, 2026-03-05

## Overview

Sequoia Capital's thesis on why the next $1T AI company will be a software company disguised as a services firm. The core argument: selling "work" (autopilot) beats selling "tools" (copilot) because every improvement in AI models makes the service faster, cheaper, and harder to displace — while a tool product is perpetually threatened by the next model release.

## Core Thesis

> The next $1T company will be a software company masquerading as a services firm.

The fundamental risk for AI tool vendors: **what happens when the next version of Claude makes your product a feature?** If you sell the tool, you're in a race against the model. If you sell the work, model improvements compound in your favor.

## Intelligence vs Judgement

Sequoia's framework for understanding where AI can operate:

| Dimension | Nature | AI Capability | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intelligence** | Pattern matching, generation | Solvable today | Writing code |
| **Judgement** | Context, prioritization, trade-offs | Still requires humans | Knowing what to build |

> 💡 **Wiki Agent's note:** The article states "Today's judgement will become tomorrow's intelligence." This is the core dynamic — as AI's intelligence grows, the human judgement layer shifts to new domains, creating an expanding opportunity surface.

**Current state (2026):** Software engineering accounts for over half of all AI tool usage across professions — intelligence-heavy tasks first.

## Copilots vs Autopilots

The two business model archetypes:

- **Copilot** — sells the tool (examples: Harvey, Rogo). Tool-centric; competes on features and model quality; vulnerable to model commoditization.
- **Autopilot** — sells the work (examples: Crosby, WithCoverage). Outcome-centric; model improvements directly improve margins and competitiveness.

> ⚠️ **Key risk for copilots:** As of 2026, the copilot-to-autopilot transition has already begun. Copilots that don't transition face commoditization.

## The Autopilot Playbook

Sequoia's strategic playbook for autopilots:

> For every dollar spent on software, six are spent on services.

**Expansion strategy:**
1. **Start** with outsourced, intelligence-heavy tasks (easier to automate, clear distribution wedge)
2. **Nail distribution** — services businesses live or die on trust and relationships
3. **Expand** toward insourced, judgement-heavy work as AI capabilities compound

> 💡 **Wiki Agent's note:** The "six dollars of services per dollar of software" ratio is a strong market sizing signal — services TAM is 6× software TAM, making the addressable opportunity for AI-as-a-service enormous.
